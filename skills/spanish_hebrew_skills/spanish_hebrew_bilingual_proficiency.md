# Bilingual Proficiency Skill: Spanish to Hebrew

## Overview
This skill provides deep knowledge of both Spanish and Hebrew languages, including vocabulary, grammar, syntax, and linguistic structures necessary for accurate translation.

## Core Competencies

### 1. Fundamental Language Differences

#### Writing System
- **Spanish:** Latin alphabet, left-to-right, 27 letters (including ñ)
- **Hebrew:** Hebrew alphabet, right-to-left, 22 letters
- **Hebrew vowels:** Nikud (vowel points) usually omitted in modern texts
- **Hebrew final forms:** 5 letters have special final forms (ך, ם, ן, ף, ץ)

#### Word Formation
- **Spanish:** Linear word construction with prefixes and suffixes
- **Hebrew:** Root-based system (shoresh - שורש)
  - Most words built from 3-letter roots
  - Roots placed in patterns (mishkalim - משקלים)
  - Example: כ-ת-ב (k-t-b) = writing-related words
    - כתב (katav) = he wrote
    - כותב (kotev) = writes/writing
    - מכתב (mikhtav) = letter
    - כתובת (ktovet) = address

### 2. Vocabulary Knowledge

#### Core Vocabulary Mappings

**Common Words:**
- sí/no → כן/לא (ken/lo)
- bueno/malo → טוב/רע (tov/ra)
- grande/pequeño → גדול/קטן (gadol/katan)
- hombre/mujer → איש/אישה (ish/isha)
- día/noche → יום/לילה (yom/laila)

**False Friends (Words that look/sound similar but differ):**
- "casual" (Spanish: casual) ≠ קז'ואל (kazual - borrowed, means casual in Hebrew too)
- "carta" (Spanish: letter/card) ≠ כרטיס (kartis - card/ticket only)
- Spanish loan words in Hebrew often from French/English, not Spanish

**Register Levels:**
- **Formal Spanish:** usted, estimado, atentos saludos
- **Formal Hebrew:** אדון/גברת (adon/gveret - Mr./Mrs.), בכבוד רב (b'khavod rav)
- **Informal Spanish:** tú, oye, tío
- **Informal Hebrew:** אתה/את (ata/at), תקשיב/י (takshiv/taKSHivi - listen)

#### Modern Hebrew Characteristics
- **Many loan words** from English, Arabic, Yiddish, Russian
- **Slang very common** in informal speech
- **Acronyms prevalent:** רמ"ח (Ramatkal - Chief of Staff), צה"ל (Tzahal - IDF)
- **New words constantly created** by Academy of Hebrew Language

### 3. Verb Systems Comparison

#### Spanish Verb Conjugation
Conjugates by: person, number, tense, mood
- Tenses: present, preterite, imperfect, future, conditional, present perfect, etc.
- Moods: indicative, subjunctive, imperative
- Regular patterns: -ar, -er, -ir verbs

#### Hebrew Verb Conjugation (Binyanim System)
**Seven verb patterns (binyanim - בניינים):**

1. **Pa'al (פעל)** - Simple active
   - כתב (katav) - he wrote
   - Pattern: simple action

2. **Nif'al (נפעל)** - Simple passive/reflexive
   - נכתב (nikhtav) - was written
   - Pattern: passive or reflexive of Pa'al

3. **Pi'el (פיעל)** - Intensive active
   - דיבר (diber) - he spoke
   - Pattern: intensive or causative

4. **Pu'al (פועל)** - Intensive passive
   - דובר (dubar) - was spoken
   - Pattern: passive of Pi'el

5. **Hitpa'el (הִתְפַּעֵל)** - Reflexive/reciprocal
   - התכתב (hitkhtev) - corresponded
   - Pattern: reflexive, reciprocal

6. **Hif'il (הִפְעִיל)** - Causative active
   - הכתיב (hiktiv) - dictated
   - Pattern: causative

7. **Huf'al (הֻפְעַל)** - Causative passive
   - הוכתב (hukhtav) - was dictated
   - Pattern: passive of Hif'il

**Conjugation by:**
- Gender (masculine/feminine)
- Number (singular/plural)
- Person (1st, 2nd, 3rd)
- Tense (past, present, future, imperative)

#### Tense Mapping

**Present Tense:**
- Spanish present → Hebrew present (הווה - hove)
- "Yo hablo" → "אני מדבר/ת" (ani medaber/medaberet)
- Hebrew present has 4 forms: m.sg, f.sg, m.pl, f.pl
- Spanish has 6 forms: yo, tú, él/ella, nosotros, vosotros, ellos

**Past Tense:**
- Spanish preterite → Hebrew past (עבר - avar)
- "Hablé" → "דיברתי" (dibarti)
- Spanish imperfect → usually Hebrew past, sometimes with היה (haya - was)
- "Hablaba" → "דיברתי" (dibarti) or "הייתי מדבר" (hayiti medaber - I was speaking)

**Future Tense:**
- Spanish future → Hebrew future (עתיד - atid)
- "Hablaré" → "אדבר" (adaber)
- Spanish conditional → Hebrew future + conditional particles
- "Hablaría" → "הייתי מדבר" (hayiti medaber) or with אם (im - if)

**Perfect Tenses:**
- Spanish present perfect → Hebrew past (no separate present perfect)
- "He hablado" → "דיברתי" (dibarti)
- Time markers indicate recency

**Progressive Forms:**
- Spanish: "Estoy hablando"
- Hebrew: Present participle serves as progressive
- "אני מדבר" (ani medaber) = I speak/I am speaking

### 4. Gender System

#### Spanish Gender
- Nouns: masculine/feminine
- Adjectives agree with nouns
- Articles: el/la, un/una
- Generally -o = masculine, -a = feminine (with exceptions)

#### Hebrew Gender
- Nouns: masculine/feminine (no neuter)
- Adjectives agree with nouns
- Verbs agree with subject gender
- No articles indicating gender, but ה (ha - the) attaches to nouns
- Gender often not predictable from form

**Gender Agreement Examples:**

Spanish: "El libro rojo" (masculine)
Hebrew: "הספר האדום" (ha-sefer ha-adom) (masculine)

Spanish: "La casa roja" (feminine)
Hebrew: "הבית האדום" (ha-bayit ha-adom) (masculine - "house" is masculine in Hebrew!)

**Important:** Many common nouns have different gender:
- "casa" (f) → "בית" bayit (m)
- "libro" (m) → "ספר" sefer (m)
- "mesa" (f) → "שולחן" shulkhan (m)
- "sol" (m) → "שמש" shemesh (f)

### 5. Plural Formation

#### Spanish Plurals
- Add -s or -es
- "libro/libros", "mujer/mujeres"
- Regular and predictable

#### Hebrew Plurals
- Masculine: usually ים- (-im)
  - ספר/ספרים (sefer/sfarim - book/books)
- Feminine: usually ות- (-ot)
  - מכונית/מכוניות (mekhonit/mekhoniyot - car/cars)
- Many irregular plurals
- Some words use both endings with meaning differences
- Dual form exists for pairs: ידיים (yadayim - two hands), עיניים (einayim - two eyes)

### 6. Definite Article

#### Spanish
- Separate words: el, la, los, las
- "el libro", "la casa"

#### Hebrew
- Prefix ה- (ha-) attached to word
- הספר (ha-sefer - the book)
- הבית (ha-bayit - the house)
- Vowel changes with first letter of word

#### Indefinite Article
- Spanish: un, una
- Hebrew: No indefinite article!
  - "un libro" → "ספר" (sefer) - just the noun
  - Context indicates definiteness

### 7. Possession

#### Spanish Possession
- Possessive adjectives: mi, tu, su, nuestro, etc.
- De + possessor: "el libro de Juan"

#### Hebrew Possession
**Method 1: Possessive Suffixes (attached to noun)**
- ספרי (sifri) - my book
- ספרך/ספרך (sifrecha/sifrech) - your book (m/f)
- ספרו (sifro) - his book
- ספרה (sifra) - her book
- ספרנו (sifrenu) - our book
- ספרכם/ספרכן (sifrechem/sifrechen) - your book (m.pl/f.pl)
- ספרם/ספרן (sifram/sifran) - their book (m/f)

**Method 2: של (shel) + possessor**
- הספר של יוחנן (ha-sefer shel Yokhanan) - John's book
- More common in modern Hebrew

**Method 3: Construct State (סמיכות - smichut)**
- ספר יוחנן (sefer Yokhanan) - John's book
- Biblical/formal style
- First noun loses definite article

### 8. Sentence Structure

#### Word Order

**Spanish:** Primarily SVO, flexible
- "Yo como manzanas" (I eat apples)
- Can omit subject: "Como manzanas"

**Hebrew:** More flexible, often VSO in formal/literary texts
- אני אוכל תפוחים (ani okhel tapukhim) - SVO
- אוכל אני תפוחים (okhel ani tapukhim) - VOS (less common)
- Modern Hebrew trending toward SVO

**Object Markers:**
- Spanish: Personal "a" before person objects
  - "Veo a María"
- Hebrew: את (et) before definite objects
  - "אני רואה את מריה" (ani ro'e et Marya)
  - את combines with definite article: את ה- (et ha-)

### 9. Prepositions

#### Common Preposition Mappings

**Spanish → Hebrew:**
- a (to) → ל- (le-) [prefix]
- de (from, of) → של (shel) [possession], מ- (mi-) [from, prefix]
- en (in) → ב- (be-) [prefix]
- con (with) → עם (im)
- para (for) → בשביל (bishvil)
- por (for, by) → על ידי (al yedei), ב- (be-)
- sin (without) → בלי (bli)
- sobre (about, on) → על (al)

**Hebrew Prefix Prepositions:**
Three letters attach directly to words:
- ב- (be-) = in, at, with
- ל- (le-) = to, for
- מ- (mi-) = from

Example: "in the house"
- Spanish: "en la casa"
- Hebrew: "בבית" (ba-bayit) - ב + ה fuses to בַּ (ba)

### 10. Pronouns

#### Subject Pronouns

**Spanish:**
- yo, tú, él/ella/usted, nosotros/as, vosotros/as, ellos/ellas/ustedes

**Hebrew:**
- אני (ani) - I
- אתה/את (ata/at) - you (m/f singular)
- הוא/היא (hu/hi) - he/she
- אנחנו (anakhnu) - we
- אתם/אתן (atem/aten) - you (m/f plural)
- הם/הן (hem/hen) - they (m/f)

**Usage:**
- Spanish often omits subject pronouns
- Hebrew typically includes them (less pro-drop than Spanish)

#### Object Pronouns

**Spanish:** Separate pronouns before verb
- "Lo veo" (I see him)

**Hebrew:** Suffix attached to verb or את (et)
- "אני רואה אותו" (ani ro'e oto) - I see him
- אותו (oto) = את + ו (him)

**Direct object markers with pronouns:**
- אותי (oti) - me
- אותך/אותך (otcha/otach) - you (m/f)
- אותו/אותה (oto/ota) - him/her
- אותנו (otanu) - us
- אותכם/אותכן (otkhem/otkhen) - you (m.pl/f.pl)
- אותם/אותן (otam/otan) - them (m/f)

### 11. Negation

#### Spanish
- "no" before verb
- "No hablo" - I don't speak
- Double negatives allowed: "No veo nada"

#### Hebrew
- לא (lo) before verb
- "אני לא מדבר" (ani lo medaber) - I don't speak
- Other negative words:
  - אין (ein) - there is no/are no
  - שום (shum) - no, none (emphatic)
  - אף (af) - not even, neither

### 12. Questions

#### Yes/No Questions

**Spanish:**
- Intonation or inversion
- "¿Hablas español?"

**Hebrew:**
- Intonation or האם (ha'im) at beginning
- "אתה מדבר עברית?" (ata medaber ivrit?)
- "האם אתה מדבר עברית?" (more formal)

#### Wh-Questions

**Spanish Question Words:**
- qué (what), quién (who), dónde (where), cuándo (when), por qué (why), cómo (how)

**Hebrew Question Words:**
- מה (ma) - what
- מי (mi) - who
- איפה (efo) - where
- מתי (matai) - when
- למה/מדוע (lama/madua) - why
- איך (eikh) - how
- כמה (kama) - how much/many
- איזה/איזו (eize/eizo) - which (m/f)

### 13. Adjectives

#### Spanish
- Usually after noun
- Must agree in gender and number
- "casa blanca" (white house)
- "libros rojos" (red books)

#### Hebrew
- After noun (like Spanish)
- Must agree in gender and number
- Takes definite article if noun is definite
- "בית לבן" (bayit lavan - white house)
- "הבית הלבן" (ha-bayit ha-lavan - THE white house)
- "ספרים אדומים" (sfarim adumim - red books)

**Key difference:** Hebrew adjective takes ה when modifying definite noun
- Spanish: "la casa blanca"
- Hebrew: "הבית הלבן" (ha-bayit ha-lavan) - both words get ה

### 14. Numbers

#### Cardinal Numbers (1-10)

**Spanish:** uno, dos, tres, cuatro, cinco, seis, siete, ocho, nueve, diez
**Hebrew:** 
- Masculine: אחד, שניים, שלושה, אַרְבָּעָה, חמישה, שישה, שבעה, שמונה, תשעה, עשרה
- Feminine: אחת, שתיים, שלוש, ארבע, חמש, שש, שבע, שמונה, תשע, עשר

**Hebrew numbers:**
- Have gender (masculine/feminine forms)
- Gender is OPPOSITE of noun! (masculine numbers with feminine nouns)
- "שלושה ספרים" (shlosha sfarim - three books, m. noun with m. number)
- "שלוש מכוניות" (shalosh mekhoniyot - three cars, f. noun with f. number)

#### Higher Numbers
- 20: veinte → עשרים (esrim)
- 100: cien → מאה (me'a)
- 1000: mil → אלף (elef)

### 15. Time Expressions

#### Clock Time
- Spanish: "Son las tres" (It's three o'clock)
- Hebrew: "השעה שלוש" (ha-sha'a shalosh - The hour is three)

#### Days of Week
- Spanish: lunes, martes, miércoles, jueves, viernes, sábado, domingo
- Hebrew: יום ראשון, יום שני, יום שלישי, יום רביעי, יום חמישי, יום שישי, שבת
  (First day, Second day... Saturday)
- Note: Week starts Sunday in Hebrew

#### Time Markers
- hoy/ayer/mañana → היום/אתמול/מחר (hayom/etmol/makhar)
- ahora/después/antes → עכשיו/אחר כך/לפני (akhshav/akhar kakh/lifnei)

### 16. Formality and Register

#### Spanish Formality
- Tú vs Usted distinction clear
- Verb forms differ
- "¿Hablas?" vs "¿Habla usted?"

#### Hebrew Formality
- Less formal register distinction
- All use same pronouns (אתה/את)
- Formality shown through:
  - Vocabulary choice
  - Titles (אדון/גברת)
  - Verb mood
  - Sentence structure
  - Plural "you" for respect: אתם (atem) for singular person (rare, very formal)

### 17. Verb Aspect Differences

#### Spanish
- Clear aspectual distinctions
- Preterite vs Imperfect
- Progressive forms (estar + gerundio)

#### Hebrew
- Less aspectual distinction
- Past tense doesn't distinguish completed vs ongoing inherently
- Progressive: present participle or היה + participle
- Context and time markers provide aspect information

### 18. Existential Constructions

#### Spanish
- "hay" for there is/are
- "Hay un libro" (There is a book)
- "Había" (There was/were)

#### Hebrew
- יש (yesh) - there is/are (present)
- היה (haya) - there was (past)
- יהיה (yihiye) - there will be (future)
- "יש ספר" (yesh sefer - There is a book)

### 19. Conditional Sentences

#### Spanish
- Si + present/past + conditional/future
- "Si llueve, me quedo" (If it rains, I stay)

#### Hebrew
- אם (im) + present/future + future/present
- "אם ירד גשם, אשאר" (im yered geshem, esh'ar)
- Less variety in conditional structures than Spanish

### 20. Relative Clauses

#### Spanish
- que, quien, cual, cuyo
- "El libro que leí" (The book that I read)

#### Hebrew
- ש- (she-) prefix attaches to following word
- "הספר שקראתי" (ha-sefer she-karati - The book that-I-read)
- Very compact compared to Spanish

### 21. Passive Voice

#### Spanish
- Ser + past participle
- Reflexive passive with "se"
- "El libro fue escrito" / "Se escribió el libro"

#### Hebrew
- Using passive binyanim (Nif'al, Pu'al, Huf'al)
- "הספר נכתב" (ha-sefer nikhtav - The book was written)
- Much more common and natural than Spanish passive

### 22. Imperatives

#### Spanish
- Special forms for tú, usted, vosotros, ustedes
- "Habla" (tú), "Hable" (usted), "Hablad" (vosotros), "Hablen" (ustedes)

#### Hebrew
- Special imperative forms
- Gender and number agreement
- "דבר/דברי" (daber/dabri - speak, m/f singular)
- "דברו" (dabru - speak, plural)

### 23. Compound Tenses

#### Spanish
- Extensive compound tenses with "haber"
- "He hablado", "Había hablado", "Habré hablado"

#### Hebrew
- No parallel compound tense system
- Use simple tenses with time markers
- Past accomplishment: simple past
- "דיברתי" (dibarti) can mean "I spoke" or "I have spoken"

### 24. Common Verb Constructions

#### Infinitive Use

**Spanish:** Common after verbs
- "Quiero comer" (I want to eat)
- "Puedo ayudar" (I can help)

**Hebrew:** Infinitive exists but less used in speech
- "אני רוצה לאכול" (ani rotze le'ekhol)
- ל + infinitive
- Often replaced by future tense: "אני רוצה שאוכל" (ani rotze she-okhal - literally "I want that I will eat")

### 25. Reflexive Verbs

#### Spanish
- Very common: levantarse, ducharse, vestirse
- "Me levanto" (I get up)

#### Hebrew
- Hitpa'el binyan serves reflexive function
- Less common than Spanish reflexive
- "אני מתלבש" (ani mitlabesh - I get dressed)
- Some Spanish reflexives = Hebrew simple verbs

## Application Guidelines

### When Translating Spanish to Hebrew

1. **Analyze Spanish structure** completely first
2. **Identify verb binyan** needed (not just tense)
3. **Check gender** (may differ between languages)
4. **Determine definiteness** (Hebrew has no indefinite article)
5. **Handle possession** (suffix vs של construction)
6. **Apply preposition prefixes** (ב, ל, מ)
7. **Position adjectives** with proper definiteness agreement
8. **Choose appropriate register** (formal/informal)
9. **Consider word order** (modern SVO vs literary VSO)

### Quality Checks

- Verify verb binyan appropriate for meaning
- Confirm gender/number agreement throughout
- Check definiteness on adjectives with definite nouns
- Validate preposition usage (prefix vs separate)
- Ensure object marker את used with definite objects
- Confirm plural forms correct (especially irregular)
- Verify possession structure appropriate
- Check that negation placed correctly

## Common Translation Patterns

### Subject Pronoun Handling
Spanish omits more than Hebrew:
- "Como" → "אני אוכל" (include אני - ani)

### Progressive Tenses
Spanish progressive → Hebrew present:
- "Estoy comiendo" → "אני אוכל" (ani okhel)

### Ser/Estar Distinction
Both → היה/הוא for past/present in Hebrew:
- "Estoy cansado" → "אני עייף" (ani ayef - no separate "estar")
- "Soy doctor" → "אני רופא" (ani rofe - no separate "ser")

### Passive Preference
Hebrew uses passive more naturally:
- "Se habla español" → "מדברים ספרדית" (medabrim sfardit) or passive binyan
